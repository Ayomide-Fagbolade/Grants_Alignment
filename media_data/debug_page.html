Botswana: Work and More Work Keeps Mma Mazile Alive

Facebook Twitter WhatsApp Flipboard LinkedIn Reddit Email Share
24 September 2009
Mmegi/The Reporter (Gaborone)

In May 2006 staff writer TSHIRELETSO MOTLOGELWA spent time with legendary farmer Mma Mazile (Chenngwe Agnes Mazile) at her farm. This week Mma Mazile sadly passed away. To mark and celebrate this vivacious and never-say-die woman we republish the feature to pay homage to this great woman

Mma Mazile (Chenngwe Agnes Mazile) is 72 going on 73, who has time to sit and calculate exactly how long she has been on this earth when there are so many more important matters to take care of? Not Mma Mazile. After all there is work to be done.

The little RAV 4 station wagon hums along the road from Palapye. She squints behind thin-rimmed glasses to get a clearer view of the road rolling towards the car. On the passenger side beside her is a take-away box of lunch she just bought an hour ago. It is still sitting there, waiting for her to get a chance and perhaps take a bite of the pasta, beef stew and the cabbage salad. "Won't the food go bad from the heat? I never get time to eat. I haven't had a bite since I woke up," she announces, smiling almost naughtily, and angling her head such that she is half looking at the road ahead and half looking behind her shoulder.

This morning at 4am - exactly the time she usually wakes up - she had contemplated making something to eat, but she realised that driving from Makoro (about 50 km away) out to the Serowe Citizen Economic Development Agency, she would need to arrive early. But the bureaucracy had kept her engaged throughout lunchtime.

She has to drive back to Makoro, to the farm, to check whether the failing deep freezer in which hundreds of chickens sit has not died again, spoiling her day. She is on her way to the farm now.

She looks like a typical Motswana granny; tukwi with the knot twisted to the side of the face, a blue leteitshi, a blouse and, for that farmer's touch, a long red coat with large white print at the back, "Naswi Makoro Farm".

Mma Mazile runs a farm about 20 km south of Palapye. It is not a farm in the normal sense of the word but rather a mammoth compound. It is a dairy project, an orange field, a vegetable field, a chicken production farm, a maize and sorghum milling plant all lumped into one in an area of a few hundred hectares. That keeps her hands pretty much full. Not that she is complaining.

She delivers a monologue on the beauty of farming. "Ngwanaka, this is not a job. A job is what you have when you can have a holiday, leave or take a sick day off. Imagine if I we were to take a day off, we would come back to find the chicks turned to biltong, like this," she says momentarily freezing her body, her face turning expressionless with mock death.

"This is not a job my son. That is the beauty of farming. It is a passion. You can't do it halfway".

Along the road comes the Foot and Mouth Prevention gate. When she dismounts from her car everyone greets her, from the police officers to the hawkers on the roadside. After the checkpoint she is back on the road. "Have you had lunch? Maybe you should have my food. I can always find food when we get to the farm," she offers, looking over her shoulder. She looks back at the road again. Her right hand taps on the wheel rhythmically. The vehicle gets off the smooth tar onto a bumpy dusty path.

After a few minutes she points to a fence in the bush, "This is where the farm starts," she explains. She starts to hum a song - something gospel-like.

Past the gate, held together loosely by a chain, a path snakes towards a few buildings scattered among the trees. Old trucks sit under the trees rusting away.

Tractor bodies and engines are scattered around the yard. Chicken scamper about. A few vans are packed under the tree shades.

Mma Mazile credits her marriage to her late husband Phenyamere Motsho Mazile in the 50s as the first step towards her farming life.

"When I got here, my husband was already into farming, although he was more interested in cattle rearing. My husband's cousin came here and said, 'This is the right place for cattle, bring them out to Marole' (a cattle post not far from Mahalapye," she explains.

When the cattle were gone, it was time to start concentrating on ploughing. One season she really, 'killed' the sorghum as they say in Setswana.

They were piles of it up to the thatching of the family's store hut. The family started selling the sorghum in the outlaying villages with payment in the form of cattle.

"We brought back many herds of cattle. That's how the cattle side of things started," she concludes. Then she remembers something. She rises up from her chair and hurries into her house. She comes back with a family album. It is an old album, almost dog-eared. She flips it open. Inside are even older photos; her husband, her 'children' in their youth - Dr Gaebulwe Mazile dancing, a teenage Tebelelo Seretse (former Minister) stirs a pot, Dr Bontshetse Mazile (academic) standing next to her in a green field.

A soft smile brightens her face; a nostalgic smile. One of her farm assistants, a mid-50s woman covered in a white powder arrives. "I checked the deep freezer it looks ok," she says. "Are you sure?" Mma Mazile asks. "Yes" "Ok. But keep checking on it. Thanks a lot," she says, her attention returning to the album. There are more photos, of 'favourite' bulls, flocks of sheep, people at wedding ceremonies. "These are students from the then (Brigade pioneer) Patrick van Rensburg's Serowe brigade who had come for tutoring," she says pointing at a few youthful faces.

She finds an old blue booklet. She flips it open and examines it carefully. "This is my Heavy-Duty licence. Given on, "she reads from the booklet, '9-10-62'. It was 1962 when I got this", she nearly brags.

She started the business of selling vegetables and fruits in the early 90s. "I would drive the truck to South Africa, buy vegetables and come back to sell them," she explains.

She had realised that the few vegetables and fruits she had planted were not enough to satisfy her hundreds of customers. "The first time I went to Johannesburg to get my supply I missed the correct turn. When I realised that I was just about flying above the market on the highway, I slowed down. I scanned both ends of the road. I waited until there was no car on sight. Soon as the last car was gone I crossed over to the opposite side. A few cars sped by blaring horns, I just kept at it until I was on the opposite side. I went back and took a left turn into the market," she narrates.

She shuts the album and goes back to her eating. She tastes the food for a few seconds and then pushes the container away.

One day on her frequent trips to South Africa she came across a dairy farm. She was fascinated. She laughs; "I saw cattle in a house. They were connected to this complex machinery, which milked them. I thought I could do that, nothing stops me doing that, so I decided I would set up a dairy. I came back and saved all I could".

When she had enough she sought the advice of a friend in the Agriculture Ministry who helped buy the right breed. Soon she was supplying milk to a number of institutions, among them Serowe Hospital, Botswana Defence Force in Paje, Swaneng School, the Teacher Training College in Serowe.

Those were the good times. But things have changed now. Deals do not come easily now and the equipment is too expensive to buy on cash, "so you have to borrow. But then you do not have constant customers to assure you of constant income".

She opens a giant gate of her milling plants. She recently bought the milling plant from Rural Industries Innovation Centre in Kanye.

"I got these with loan money from CEDA. That is why I had to rush to those people this morning. It's difficult making monthly payments but it has to be done," she says while running her fingers over a little pile of maize meal. And the chicken business is not doing good either because all other forces have started to come into play regarding tenders.

She thinks government could help by putting mechanisms in place that would ensure that government institution buy their supplies from local farmers than from multinational corporation-run wholesalers. When her husband passed away last year in April, she felt that it would get tougher making it in the farm. But she won't be defeated.

It is way past sunset. "You will not leave this place without having anything to eat," she declares sitting down after a tour of the farm.

It is already about 8pm when the dinner arrives. Her farm assistant walks towards her with a bowl to wash her hands. She shakes her head. "Serve the visitors first," she says bemusedly. After making sure that everyone is eating, she is ready to have her share. She slowly washes her hands and starts feeling the bogobe porridge.

It is that rare moment when she gets to sit down and she seems restless, looking here and there, scanning the equipment, the trucks, the houses and the cat frolicking on the dusty ground.

"The only thing that keeps me going is work. If there was no work I would have no reason to live. Work is the only reason why we live," she says.
